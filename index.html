10-K Annual Report Annual Reports
The definitive annual report for US public companies. It contains audited financial statements including the Balance Sheet, Income Statement, and Cash Flow Statement. Crucially, it includes the MD&A (Item 7), where management explains the 'why' behind the numbers, and Item 1A (Risk Factors), detailing everything that could go wrong. It also covers legal proceedings, executive compensation, and the effectiveness of internal controls (SOX 404).
All domestic public companies registered under Exchange Act Sections 12 or 15(d).
60 days (large accelerated), 75 days (accelerated), or 90 days (non-accelerated) after fiscal year-end.
The 10-K is the 'gold standard' for fundamental analysis. It's used to build discounted cash flow (DCF) models, assess long-term competitive moats, and verify the integrity of reported earnings via the auditor's report. Professional analysts skip the investor presentation and go straight to the 10-K to find hidden liabilities, pension obligations, and lease commitments that aren't visible in summary data.
20-F Annual Report — Foreign Private Issuers Annual Reports
The foreign private issuer's equivalent of a 10-K. It is the primary annual disclosure for non-US companies listed on US exchanges (ADRs). It features audited financials, often under IFRS, and includes a critical mandatory reconciliation to US GAAP for certain items. It also covers business risks, governance, and director backgrounds for international entities like ASML, Alibaba, or Sony.
 Foreign private issuers (FPIs) listed on US exchanges. Within 120 days (4 months) after fiscal year-end.
The only audited source for ADR stock financials. It allows investors to compare foreign giants against US domestic peers by normalizing financial reporting. Analysts look for unique international risk factors (currency, geopolitical, foreign tax) and 'Material Weaknesses' in internal controls that are more common in non-US firms.
40-F Annual Report — Canadian Companies (MJDS) Annual Reports
Annual report for Canadian companies using the Multijurisdictional Disclosure System. Allows use of Canadian disclosure documents accepted by SEC.
 Qualifying Canadian companies listed on US exchanges under MJDS.
Same day as the issuer's annual report is due to be filed with Canadian securities regulators.
Source of annual financials for major Canadian companies trading on US exchanges (e.g., Shopify, TD Bank, Canadian Natural Resources).
18-K Annual Report — Foreign Governments Annual Reports
Annual report filed by foreign governments and their agencies that issue securities in the US (e.g., sovereign bonds). Includes macroeconomic data, debt levels, and government finances.
Foreign governments, central banks, and government agencies that have issued securities in US markets.
 Typically within 9 months after fiscal year-end.
Used by sovereign bond investors and EM (emerging market) debt analysts to assess country credit risk, GDP, debt-to-GDP ratios, and fiscal position.
11-K Annual Report — Employee Stock Plans Annual Reports
Annual report specifically for employee stock purchase plans, 401(k) plans, or savings plans that invest in company securities. Contains audited plan financials.
Companies whose employee benefit/savings plans hold company securities registered under the Securities Act.
Within 90 days after plan's fiscal year-end (180 days for plans subject to ERISA).
Used in equity compensation analysis. Reveals actual shares held by employees in company stock, a proxy for internal confidence in the stock.
10-Q Quarterly Report Quarterly Reports
A quarterly report filed after each of the first three fiscal quarters. Unlike the 10-K, current quarterly financial statements are typically unaudited. It provides a real-time update on a company's financial health, seasonal trends, and any material developments that occurred since the last annual report. It includes condensed financials and a shorter MD&A focused specifically on the recent quarter's performance.
 All domestic Exchange Act reporting companies.
40 days (large accelerated/accelerated) or 45 days (non-accelerated) after each of first three fiscal quarter-ends.
Investors use 10-Qs to track short-term momentum and identify 'earnings beats' or 'misses' early. It reveals deteriorating margins or slowing growth trends months before the annual report. Comparing the 10-Q to the previous year's corresponding quarter (YoY) is the standard method for measuring fundamental growth in public markets.
8-K Current Report — Material Events Current / Event Reports
The 'breaking news' filing. Companies must file an 8-K for 'material' events, often within 4 business days. This includes earnings releases (Item 2.02), entering into major contracts (Item 1.01), CEO or CFO departures (Item 5.02), bankruptcy, or dividend changes. It is the primary vehicle for ensuring all investors have access to market-moving information simultaneously.
 All domestic Exchange Act reporting companies. Within 4 business days of the triggering event.
An 8-K is the ultimate catalyst for stock price volatility. Day traders and institutional desks monitor 8-K feeds (Item 99.1 exhibits) for earnings slides and press releases. A sudden 8-K filing can signal a company is about to be acquired, is facing a major lawsuit, or is experiencing a management crisis. It is the most time-sensitive filing on EDGAR.
6-K Foreign Issuer Current Report Current / Event Reports
The 'current report' for foreign private issuers (the international version of an 8-K). It is used to report any material information that a foreign company releases in its home market, to its local exchange, or to its shareholders. This includes everything from quarterly results and debt issuances to major executive changes and M&A activity.
 Foreign private issuers registered under Section 12 of the Exchange Act. Promptly after information is made public in home country.
Essential for tracking the 'intra-year' progress of ADRs. Because foreign companies don't file 10-Qs, the 6-K is the only way to get quarterly or semi-annual performance updates. It is the definitive record of how a foreign company is interacting with its global shareholder base.
1-U Current Report — Regulation A Issuers Current / Event Reports
Current report for companies that went public via Regulation A offering (Tier 2). Discloses material events similar to an 8-K but for smaller Reg A companies.
 Companies with ongoing Reg A Tier 2 offerings or reporting obligations. Within 4 business days of triggering event.
Tracks material news for smaller, non-NYSE/NASDAQ companies that used Reg A mini-IPO route. Niche but important for micro-cap and retail crowdfunding investors.
S-1 IPO Registration Statement Registration Statements (S-Series)
The 'Initial Public Offering' (IPO) registration statement. It is the first time a private company pulls back the curtain, disclosing 3 years of audited financials, the full competitive landscape, and every conceivable risk factor. It includes the 'Use of Proceeds' section, explaining how the company plans to spend the IPO cash.
 Domestic companies conducting first public offering.
Filed before IPO; effective after SEC review. Must be public at least 15 days before roadshow begins.
The S-1 is the definitive guide to a new stock. It helps investors determine if an IPO is a 'hype' play or a sustainable business. Analysts focus on the revenue growth rates, path to profitability, and the 'Dual-Class' share structure which often gives founders total voting control even with a minority of shares.
S-3 Shelf Registration Statement Registration Statements (S-Series)
The 'Shelf Registration' statement for seasoned companies. It allows a company to register a large amount of securities (stock, bonds, warrants) in advance and then 'pull them off the shelf' to sell to the public over the next 3 years. It is a short-form registration that incorporates previous filings by reference.
 Large public companies with at least 12 months of Exchange Act reporting. Effective immediately for WKSIs; others require SEC review.
Every S-3 filing is a potential dilution signal. It means the company is preparing to raise capital. Investors track the 'Shelf Overhang' to see how much new stock could suddenly hit the market. It is the primary vehicle for ATM (At-the-Market) offerings, which allow companies to sell shares directly into daily trading volume.
S-4 Business Combination Registration Registration Statements (S-Series)
The 'Merger & Acquisition' registration statement. Filed when a company issues new shares to acquire another business. It contains the most detailed information available on a corporate deal, including the 'Background of the Merger' (a secret history of the negotiations) and 'Pro Forma' financials showing what the combined company will look like.
 Acquirer companies using stock consideration in M&A. Filed in connection with the deal; effective before shareholder vote.
The deal-maker's bible. Risk arbitrageurs and M&A analysts use the S-4 to determine if a deal is actually good for shareholders. It reveals the 'Fairness Opinion' from investment banks and explains exactly how much dilution current shareholders will face in the acquisition.
S-8 Employee Benefit Plan Registration Registration Statements (S-Series)
A registration statement for securities offered to employees, consultants, or advisors under an employee benefit plan (like an ESOP or 401k). It is used to register shares for stock options and restricted stock units (RSUs).
 Public companies registering equity compensation plan shares. Effective immediately upon filing.
Provides a view into the company's employee incentive structure. A large S-8 filing indicates that a significant number of shares are being authorized for employee compensation, which informs analysts about potential future dilution as those options vest and are exercised.
S-8 POS Post-Effective Amendment — Employee Benefit Plan Registration Statements (S-Series)
Post-effective amendment to an S-8 to update information about an employee benefit plan.
 Same company that filed original S-8. Filed as needed after S-8 effectiveness.
Tracks updates to equity compensation structures ΓÇö changes to option pool size, new plan adoptions.
S-11 Registration — Real Estate Companies Registration Statements (S-Series)
Registration statement for REITs and companies primarily engaged in real estate operations. Similar to S-1 but tailored for real estate entities and their unique financial metrics.
 REITs and real estate companies offering securities. Filed before public offering; effective upon SEC declaration.
Primary registration document for REIT IPOs. Contains property portfolio details, FFO projections, occupancy rates, and lease expirations ΓÇö all critical REIT-specific metrics.
S-4MEF S-4 Additional Securities (Rule 462(b)) Registration Statements (S-Series) Adds securities to a prior S-4 registration in a business combination. Companies issuing additional shares in M&A transactions. Filed as needed in connection with the business combination.
Signals expanded share issuance in M&A ΓÇö may affect the exchange ratio calculation and deal economics.
SB-2 Small Business Registration (Discontinued) Registration Statements (S-Series)
Optional registration form for small businesses (revenue under $25M) to register securities for public sale. Discontinued in 2008.
 Small businesses (pre-2008). Discontinued Feb 2008. Historical filings only.
Historical IPO database entry point for small-cap stocks that went public before 2008.
F-1 IPO Registration — Foreign Private Issuers Registration Statements (F-Series)
The registration statement for foreign private issuers going public in the US for the first time. It is the international version of the S-1. It provides 3 years of audited financials, business history, and an exhaustive list of risk factors specific to the company's home country and the US listing.
 Foreign companies listing on US exchanges for the first time. Filed before US IPO; effective upon SEC declaration.
Crucial for analyzing the 'Cross-Border IPO.' It reveals the capital structure of the foreign entity and how the US ADRs will be backed by the underlying foreign shares. Essential for determining if a high-growth international company has the governance standards necessary for a US listing.
F-3 Shelf Registration — Foreign Private Issuers Registration Statements (F-Series)
Shelf registration for seasoned foreign private issuers. Allows rapid secondary offerings and debt issuances over 3 years.
 Seasoned FPIs with at least 12 months of Exchange Act reporting. Effective immediately for WKSIs; others require SEC review.
Dilution monitoring for ADR stocks. Same function as S-3 but for foreign companies. Tracked when large ADR issuers raise capital in US markets.
F-4 Business Combination Registration — Foreign Issuers Registration Statements (F-Series)
Registration of securities issued by foreign private issuers in business combinations, mergers, and exchange offers.
 Foreign private issuers using stock consideration in M&A. Filed in connection with transaction; effective before shareholder vote.
Used in cross-border M&A where a foreign company acquires a US company (or vice versa) using shares. Contains pro-forma combined financials for cross-border deal analysis.
F-6 ADR Registration Statement Registration Statements (F-Series)
Registers American Depositary Receipts (ADRs) representing securities of foreign private issuers. Each ADR corresponds to a specific number of underlying foreign shares.
 Depositary banks (e.g., JPMorgan, Citibank) establishing ADR programs. Filed before ADR program commences.
Establishes the legal framework for ADR trading. The F-6 contains the ADR ratio (e.g., 1 ADR = 10 ordinary shares) ΓÇö critical for calculating true per-share metrics.
F-7 Rights Offering — Canadian Issuers Registration Statements (F-Series)
Registration for Canadian issuers offering securities to existing US shareholders via rights offering.
 Canadian issuers conducting rights offerings to US shareholders. Filed before rights offering commences.
Rights offerings create arbitrage opportunities between the subscription price and market price. Used by event-driven investors.
F-8 Exchange Offer — Canadian Issuers Registration Statements (F-Series)
Registration for securities of Canadian issuers issued in exchange offers or business combinations with other Canadian companies.
 Canadian issuers in exchange offer transactions. Filed before the exchange offer commences.
Cross-border M&A tracking tool for Canadian company deals involving US-listed shares.
F-9 Debt Securities — Canadian Issuers Registration Statements (F-Series) Registration of investment-grade debt securities by Canadian issuers under MJDS. Canadian issuers registering investment-grade debt in US markets. Filed before the offering.
Tracks Canadian corporate bond issuances in US markets ΓÇö relevant for fixed income investors monitoring Canadian corporate credit.
F-10 Securities Registration — Canadian Issuers Registration Statements (F-Series)
General securities registration for Canadian issuers under MJDS. Similar to S-1 but allows use of Canadian disclosure documents.
 Canadian companies registering securities (equity or debt) in US markets. Filed before public offering.
Tracks Canadian company capital market activities in the US ΓÇö used by cross-border portfolio managers.
10-12B Exchange Act Registration — Section 12(b) Registration Statements (Other)
Initial registration of a class of securities on a national securities exchange (NYSE, NASDAQ) under Section 12(b). The form that makes a company an Exchange Act reporting company when listing on a major exchange.
Companies listing on national exchanges that were not previously SEC reporting companies.
 Filed before or upon listing on a national exchange.
Marks the moment a company becomes a full SEC reporting issuer ΓÇö often filed by direct listings, spin-offs, or emerging companies becoming exchange-listed.
10-12G Exchange Act Registration — Section 12(g) Registration Statements (Other)
Registration of a class of securities under Section 12(g) by companies with 2,000+ shareholders (or 500 non-accredited investors) and total assets exceeding $10 million that are not exchange-listed. Triggers Exchange Act reporting obligations.
 Companies crossing Section 12(g) registration thresholds. Filed when shareholder thresholds are crossed.
Tracks companies that become public reporters without doing a traditional IPO. Important for OTC market research and tracking companies that grow into SEC reporting requirements.
8-A12B Securities Registration — Section 12(b) Registration Statements (Other)
Short-form registration of a class of securities under Section 12(b) for companies already registered under the Exchange Act that list on a national exchange.
 Already-reporting companies listing a new class of securities on an exchange. Filed when securities are listed on a national exchange.
Tracks when a company lists new securities classes (e.g., preferred stock) on an exchange ΓÇö important for preferred stock investors.
8-A12G Successor Registration — Section 12(g) Registration Statements (Other)
Notification that a successor issuer's securities are deemed registered under Section 12(g) following a corporate succession.
 Successor companies in mergers or restructurings. Filed in connection with succession event.
Corporate identity tracking ΓÇö ensures continuity of reporting obligations after corporate restructurings.
18-12B Securities Registration — Section 12(b) (Foreign Govts) Registration Statements (Other)
Registration of securities of foreign governments or their agencies under Section 12(b).
 Foreign governments issuing exchange-listed securities in the US. Before listing on a US exchange.
Relevant for sovereign bond investors tracking government-issued securities listed on US exchanges.
DEF 14A Definitive Proxy Statement Proxy Statements
The 'Proxy Statement' for the annual shareholder meeting. This is where you find the 'real' story of corporate governance. It details the Summary Compensation Table (what the CEO actually made), the background of board members, and any shareholder proposals on the ballot. It also discloses 'Related Person Transactions' where the company might be doing business with an insider's family members.
 All public companies holding shareholder meetings. Mailed or made available at least 40 calendar days before the annual meeting.
Essential for ESG (Environmental, Social, and Governance) analysis. It reveals if a CEO's pay is aligned with performance or if the board is 'entrenched' with long-tenured members who lack independence. Activist campaigns often start by analyzing the DEF 14A for governance weaknesses and excessive perk spending.
DEFN14A Definitive Proxy — Non-Management Proxy Statements
Definitive proxy statement filed by a party other than management ΓÇö typically an activist shareholder running a competing slate of directors.
 Non-management parties soliciting proxies (activists, dissident shareholders). Before the shareholder meeting.
The activist's own proxy ΓÇö presents the dissident case for change. Often proposes new directors, demands strategic review, or pushes for sale of the company.
PX14A6G Exempt Solicitation Notice Proxy Statements
Filed by large shareholders (owning over $5M in company securities) who communicate with other shareholders about proxy votes without formally soliciting proxies ΓÇö exempt from full proxy rules.
 Large shareholders ($5M+) communicating about proxy issues. Promptly when exempt soliciting materials are distributed.
Tracks major institutional views on contested governance issues without full proxy filing overhead. Shows which large shareholders are taking public positions on proxy votes.
424B4 Final Prospectus — Rule 424(b)(4) Prospectuses (424 / 497 Series)
The 'Final Prospectus' filed after a registration statement (like an S-1 or F-1) has become effective. It is the final version of the IPO or secondary offering document, including the actual final offering price, the exact number of shares sold, and the list of participating underwriters.
 All companies pricing a registered public offering. Filed within 2 business days of pricing.
The definitive document for any new stock offering. Unlike the 'Preliminary' (Red Herring) prospectus, the 424B4 is the legally binding final version. Analysts use it to confirm the final valuation and to track the 'Lock-Up' agreements that prevent insiders from selling shares immediately after the IPO.
425 Merger/Acquisition Communications Prospectuses (424 / 497 Series)
Written communications relating to business combinations (mergers, tender offers) filed during the solicitation period. Includes investor presentations, press releases, and factual business information distributed about the deal.
 Companies and advisers communicating about pending M&A transactions. Filed on the date the communication is made.
M&A deal marketing materials ΓÇö investor presentations arguing why the deal creates value. Used by merger arbitrageurs to track deal narrative, management guidance, and deal progress.
497 Mutual Fund Prospectus Prospectuses (424 / 497 Series)
Final prospectus for an open-end investment company (mutual fund). Contains the fund's investment objective, fees, performance history, and portfolio manager details.
 Open-end management investment companies (mutual funds). Filed within 5 business days after fund effectiveness.
The investor disclosure document for mutual funds. Fee comparison, performance track record, and manager tenure all found here. Used by fund research platforms like Morningstar.
497K Mutual Fund Summary Prospectus Prospectuses (424 / 497 Series)
Condensed summary prospectus for mutual funds ΓÇö a shorter, more investor-friendly version covering key fund facts: fees, performance, risks.
 Mutual funds providing a summary prospectus to investors. Filed simultaneously with or before the full 497 prospectus.
The key facts document that fund comparison sites display ΓÇö expense ratio, fund objective, performance, and risk summary all derived from 497K.
4 Statement of Changes in Ownership Insider Trading (Section 16)
A report of a change in ownership for corporate insiders (officers, directors, or 10% owners). Most Form 4 filings report open-market buys or sells, but they also cover stock option exercises and RSU vestings. Under SEC rules, these must be filed within 2 business days of the trade.
 All corporate insiders: officers, directors, 10%+ shareholders. Within 2 business days of the transaction.
The most powerful insider signal. While insiders might sell for many reasons (taxes, diversifying, buying a house), they almost always buy for only one: they think the price is going up. 'Cluster Buying'—where multiple insiders buy stock simultaneously—is one of the most reliable indicators of future outperformance.
SC 13D Schedule 13D — Activist Beneficial Ownership Beneficial Ownership (13D/G)
The Activist Investor's calling card. Filed when an investor (or group) acquires more than 5% of a company with the intent to influence or change control. The most critical section is Item 4: Purpose of Transaction, where the activist must disclose if they want to fire the CEO, sell the company, or demand board seats. Any change in 'intent' requires a prompt amendment.
 Any investor or group acquiring 5%+ beneficial ownership with activist intent. Within 5 business days of crossing the 5% threshold.
A 13D filing is a massive bullish signal. When institutional 'raiders' like Elliott Management or Carl Icahn file a 13D, it implies they believe the stock is undervalued and they will fight to unlock that value. Following 13D filings allows retail investors to 'ride the coattails' of the world's most aggressive and successful hedge fund managers.
13F-HR Quarterly Institutional Portfolio Holdings Institutional Holdings (13F)
A quarterly window into the portfolios of the world's largest investment managers (those with $100M+ in US equities). It lists all long positions, including share counts and market values. High-frequency 13Fs (like those from Renaissance Technologies) or value-focused 13Fs (like Berkshire Hathaway) are dissected by the entire financial community.
Investment managers exercising investment discretion over $100M+ in US equity securities.
Within 45 days after each calendar quarter-end (February 14, May 15, August 14, November 14).
13Fs allow you to see what 'The Smart Money' is doing. You can track which sectors managers are rotating into or witness the exact moment Warren Buffett starts selling a position. Many retail strategies involve 'Copycat Investing,' which replicates the top holdings of elite hedge funds based on their 13F disclosures.
13F-NT 13F Notice — No Holdings Institutional Holdings (13F)
Filed when a manager required to file 13F has no holdings to report ΓÇö all positions were liquidated or transferred.
 Institutional managers with 13F obligations but zero reportable holdings. Same deadline as 13F-HR: within 45 days of quarter-end.
Signals when a major fund has completely exited all US equity positions ΓÇö rare but significant. Can signal fund closure, strategy change, or transition to non-reportable assets.
13F-E 13F Amendment Institutional Holdings (13F)
Amendment to a previously filed 13F-HR. Filed when a manager discovers errors in reported holdings.
 Institutional managers correcting 13F-HR filings. As needed after original 13F-HR.
Corrects institutional ownership data ΓÇö important for databases tracking precise fund position sizes and changes over time.
SC TO-T Tender Offer — Third-Party Acquirer Tender Offers & M&A
Filed when a third party launches a formal tender offer to purchase another company's shares directly from shareholders at a specified premium price. Includes offer price, conditions, financing, minimum shares needed, and plans for target post-acquisition.
 Third-party companies or investors making tender offers. Filed on the date the tender offer commences.
Acquisition announcement document. Tender offers typically offer 20—50% premiums ΓÇö immediate price catalyst. Yahoo Finance, MarketBeat, and Seeking Alpha headline SC TO-T filings as M&A breaking news. Merger arb funds analyze offer conditions and bump probability from this filing.
SC TO-T/A Amendment to Third-Party Tender Offer Tender Offers & M&A
Amendment to update terms of a pending tender offer ΓÇö typically to extend the deadline, increase the offer price, waive conditions, or respond to competing bids.
 Third-party offerors amending their tender offer. As material changes occur during the offer period.
Offer bump tracker. SC TO-T/A increasing the price is a direct arbitrage opportunity signal. Extension amendments signal regulatory delays. Price bumps after SC TO-T/A are among the cleanest merger arb setups.
SC TO-I Tender Offer — Issuer Self-Tender Tender Offers & M&A
Filed when a company makes a tender offer to repurchase its own shares at a premium to market price. A more aggressive form of share buyback than open-market repurchase programs.
 Public companies buying back their own shares via formal tender offer. Filed when the self-tender commences.
Premium buyback signal ΓÇö management is so confident the stock is undervalued they're offering a premium to buy it back. Strong capital return and undervaluation signal tracked by MarketBeat and Yahoo Finance.
SC TO-I/A Amendment to Issuer Self-Tender Tender Offers & M&A Amendment to an issuer's self-tender offer, updating terms, price, or deadline. Companies amending their self-tender offers. As material changes occur during self-tender.
Tracks changes to buyback tender terms ΓÇö price increases are additional positive capital return signals.
SC TO-C Tender Offer Written Communications Tender Offers & M&A
Written communications about a tender offer made before or during the offer period ΓÇö investor presentations, press releases, and deal-related communications.
 Offerors making pre-offer or during-offer written communications. Filed on the date the communication is made.
M&A deal communications tracking. Shows how management and acquirer are framing the deal narrative to shareholders ΓÇö used in contested bid situations to track changing deal rationale.
CB Cross-Border Tender Offer / Rights Offering Tender Offers & M&A
Used for tender offers, exchange offers, rights offerings, and business combinations where the target is a foreign private issuer with less than 10% US shareholders. Allows use of home-country documents.
Parties in cross-border offers where the target FPI has minimal US shareholder base.
 Filed at commencement of the offer.
Tracks cross-border M&A deals where the target is a foreign company. Used by international merger arbitrage strategies.
SC 13E-3 Going-Private Transaction Statement Going Private
Required for any transaction by a public company or its affiliates that would result in the company being deregistered. Requires detailed fairness analysis, financial projections, and justification that the transaction is fair to unaffiliated shareholders. The most shareholder-protective filing requirement.
Controlling shareholders, management teams, or companies in take-private transactions.
 Filed before the going-private transaction is sent to shareholders.
Minority shareholder protection document. The financial projections disclosed here often reveal management's true view of company value ΓÇö sometimes far higher than the take-private offer. Activist investors and litigation lawyers scrutinize 13E-3 filings intensively.
25 Exchange Delisting Notice Deregistration & Delisting
Filed by the national securities exchange notifying the SEC that it is delisting a security. The exchange's official notice that a stock is being removed from its listing.
National securities exchanges (NYSE, NASDAQ, NYSE American) delisting a security.
 Filed when the exchange delists the security.
Official delisting confirmation. After a Form 25 is filed, the stock typically moves to OTC markets or ceases trading entirely. The most definitive delisting signal in EDGAR.
1-A Regulation A Offering Statement Regulation A / Crowdfunding
Offering statement for Regulation A mini-IPOs. Tier 1 allows up to $20M; Tier 2 allows up to $75M per 12-month period from non-accredited investors. Requires an offering circular similar to a simplified prospectus. Qualified by SEC (not declared effective).
 US or Canadian companies conducting Regulation A mini-public offerings. Filed before commencing offering; qualified by SEC (typically 60—90 day review).
Mini-IPO tracking. Companies too small for traditional NASDAQ/NYSE IPO use Reg A. Popular on retail investment platforms like Republic, Wefunder, and StartEngine. Creates early-stage investment opportunities before possible uplist to major exchanges.
C Regulation Crowdfunding Offering Statement Regulation A / Crowdfunding
Offering statement for Regulation Crowdfunding (Reg CF) transactions through SEC-registered funding portals. Allows companies to raise up to $5M from the general public.
US companies raising money through Reg CF via registered crowdfunding portals (Wefunder, Republic, StartEngine).
 Filed with SEC before offering commences.
Crowdfunding offering disclosure. Contains business plan, financials, and use of proceeds for companies raising from retail crowdfunding investors. Used by portals to display investment opportunity details.
D Notice of Exempt Offering — Regulation D Exempt Offerings (Reg D)
Notice of a private securities offering conducted under Regulation D exemptions (Rules 504, 506(b), 506(c)). Not a registration ΓÇö just a notice to the SEC that a private offering is happening. Used for EVERY venture capital, private equity, and hedge fund raise.
Companies, funds, and startups raising money under Regulation D from accredited investors.
 Within 15 calendar days after the first sale of securities.
The primary signal of private capital raises. EDGAR Form D filings reveal: which companies are raising, how much, at what price, and who is managing the deal. Venture capital databases and private market trackers (Pitchbook, Crunchbase) supplement their data with Form D disclosures.
D/A Amendment to Form D Exempt Offerings (Reg D)
Amendment to update a previously filed Form D. Required for material changes to offering terms and annually for ongoing Reg D offerings.
 Companies and funds that filed the original Form D.
Within 15 days of material change; annually within 15 days of one-year anniversary.
Tracks ongoing private capital raise activity ΓÇö total raised, new investors, changed terms. Venture investors track D/A amendments to follow fundraising progress of portfolio companies.
N-1A Mutual Fund Registration Statement Investment Companies (N-Series)
The registration form used by open-end management investment companies (Mutual Funds). It contains the fund's investment objectives, strategies, risks, and a detailed fee table. It is the source document for the fund's statutory prospectus.
Open-end management investment companies (mutual funds) offering shares to the public.
 Initial registration before offering shares; annual post-effective amendments.

In-Depth Education

Deep-Dive Filing Guides

Detailed explanations of each major SEC form — what it is, what it contains, deadlines, and why investors should care.

Form 10-K — Annual Report

Form 10-K is the annual report providing a comprehensive analysis of a company's financial condition. Though it overlaps with a company's glossy "annual report," the 10-K is the longer, more detailed technical document filed within 60–90 days of the fiscal year end.

Key Sections

  • Business Summary: Operations, segments, products, subsidiaries, markets, regulatory issues, R&D, competition, and employees.
  • Cybersecurity: Disclosures regarding cybersecurity risk management processes, strategy, and governance (mandatory since Dec 2023).
  • Management Discussion & Analysis (MD&A): Management explains operations and financial results for the past year.
  • Financial Statements: Audited balance sheet, income statement, and cash flow statement for fundamental analysis.
  • Risk Factors: Material risks such as legal proceedings, competitive landscape, regulatory changes, and market conditions.

Why Investors Read the 10-K

The 10-K is the primary document for fundamental analysis — extracting revenues, earnings, growth, ROE, profit margins, and equity multiples to determine underlying value. For corporate bonds, look at liquidity, leverage, and solvency ratios.

Pay attention to Item 1 (what the company does), risk factors, and especially footnotes — they explain accounting methods and can flag questionable practices, prior errors, legal cases, and synthetic leases. (Note: What was formerly Item 6 "Selected Financial Data" was eliminated in 2021 and is now reserved).

Tip: As an investor, pay special attention to footnotes in the 10-K to flag questionable accounting practices. Very confusing footnote sections or sudden one-time charges can be red flags.

Form 10-Q — Quarterly Report

Form 10-Q is a truncated version of the 10-K filed quarterly for the first three quarters of the fiscal year. The deadline is 40–45 days from the end of the quarter. Unlike the 10-K, financial statements are unaudited and less detailed.

What's Inside

  • Condensed unaudited financial statements (income statement, balance sheet, cash flow)
  • MD&A for the quarter
  • Updates to risk factors and legal proceedings
  • Changes in securities and use of proceeds

Why Investors Rely on the 10-Q

Because the 10-K only comes once a year, the 10-Q lets investors update valuation metrics and financial ratios quarterly. Key areas include changes to working capital, accounts receivable, inventory factors, share buybacks, and legal risks.

Investor tip: Compare a company's 10-Q with a close competitor's to evaluate relative performance — strengths, weaknesses, and room for improvement.

Form 8-K — Current Report

The 8-K discloses major developments between 10-K and 10-Q filings. Triggers include bankruptcies, material impairments, acquisitions/dispositions, and executive changes. Companies must generally file within four business days of the event.

Common 8-K Triggers

  • Mergers, acquisitions, or asset dispositions
  • Bankruptcy or receivership
  • CEO/CFO departure or appointment
  • Material impairments or write-downs
  • Amendments to articles of incorporation
  • Material Cybersecurity Incidents
  • Earnings announcements (voluntary)

Impact on Investors

The 8-K provides direct, timely notification not filtered by media or sell-side analysts. Because 8-K disclosures are legally standardized and must be accurate, they provide a complete record useful for event studies and statistical research on stock price impacts.

Form S-1 — IPO Registration Statement

A company preparing to offer securities to the public for the first time files a Form S-1 registration statement. It consists of two parts:

  • Prospectus: Mandatory document for every buyer — details management, business operations, financial health, results, risk factors. The Securities Act of 1933 requires this. Financials must be audited by an independent CPA.
  • Additional Information: Recent sales of unregistered securities, material contracts, and other relevant data.

Reading the Prospectus

Focus on company-specific or unique information rather than broad legal statements. Look for risk factors, use of proceeds, competitive landscape, and growth strategy. Because the prospectus is a legal declaration with transparency standards, companies must include certain facts — though they may use careful wording to minimize red flags.

Important: Forward-looking statements in the prospectus are only projections. There is no guarantee the company will meet its targets for sales and profits.

Form 13F — Institutional Holdings

Form 13F is filed quarterly by institutional investment managers with discretionary equity holdings exceeding $100 million. Filers include hedge funds, mutual funds, banks, insurance companies, and pension funds. Due within 45 calendar days after each quarter end.

What It Reveals

  • Complete list of equity positions (stocks and options)
  • Number of shares held and market value
  • Changes in positions from prior quarter (new buys, additions, reductions, sells)

How Investors Use 13F

Investors track "smart money" by monitoring what top hedge funds and institutional managers are buying and selling. 13F filings reveal portfolio strategies, sector bets, and conviction levels. However, note the 45-day delay means positions may have changed by the time the filing is public.

Warning: 13F filings only show long equity positions. They do not disclose short positions, most derivatives, fixed income, or non-U.S. securities, so the picture is incomplete.

Proxy Statement (DEF 14A)

The proxy statement is presented before the annual shareholder meeting and must be filed with the SEC before soliciting votes. It reveals management salaries, perks, and all items up for shareholder vote.

What's Inside

  • Executive and director compensation (salary, bonus, stock awards, total)
  • Board nominee biographies and qualifications
  • Items to be voted on (director elections, compensation plans, auditor ratification, Say-on-Pay, and Say-on-Frequency votes)
  • How to vote: by mail, phone, or online — deadline is typically 24 hours before the meeting

Importance for Shareholders

Owning common stock gives you a vote (usually one per share). The proxy statement lets you cast your votes through a designated proxy who aggregates and casts votes on your behalf. Vote options: "For," "Against," "Abstain," or "Not Voted."

Forms 3, 4, and 5 — Insider Ownership

The SEC defines an insider as officers, directors, and beneficial owners of more than 10% of a class of equity securities. These individuals must report their ownership and transactions:

  • Form 3: Initial statement of beneficial ownership — filed within 10 days of becoming an insider.
  • Form 4: Report of changes in ownership — filed within 2 business days of any transaction.
  • Form 5: Annual summary of Form 4 and any transactions that should have been reported.

Why Track Insider Activity

If insiders are buying shares, they might know something the market doesn't — great potential, a possible acquisition, or undervaluation. Peter Lynch: "There's only one reason insiders buy: they think the stock is undervalued." Insiders are prevented from short-swing trading (profiting from matching buys and sells) within any rolling six-month period.

Warning: Too much insider ownership can be a governance risk. When insiders gain corporate control, management may not feel responsible to shareholders.

Schedule 13D — Beneficial Ownership Report

Required when any person or group acquires more than 5% of voting shares with active intent. Initial filings are due within 5 business days, and material-change amendments are generally due within 2 business days. Schedule 13G is the short-form alternative for eligible passive/QII/exempt filers.

Disclosure Requirements

  • Acquirer's name, address, and background
  • Relationship with the company
  • Purpose of the transaction
  • Type and class of security
  • Source of funds used for purchases
  • Criminal history in the past five years

Added through the 1968 Williams Act to warn investors of impending changes to corporate control from activist investors or corporate raiders. Investors use 13D both to detect governance risks and as a possible harbinger of acquisition.

Form 144 — Restricted Stock Sale Notice

Form 144 notifies intent to sell restricted stock — typically acquired in non-public transactions. Required for affiliates (insiders) when sales in any three-month period exceed 5,000 shares or $50,000 (non-affiliates are exempt). The sale must occur within 90 days of filing.

Lock-Up Periods

After an IPO, insiders sign lock-up agreements (typically 180 days, ranging 90–180 days) prohibiting stock sales. Form 144 is useful for knowing how much supply may hit the market when lock-ups expire — potentially affecting stock price.

Form 20-F — Foreign Annual Report

Form 20-F is the annual report filed by foreign private issuers listed on U.S. exchanges. It's similar to the 10-K for domestic companies, covering financials, governance, business operations, and risk factors. Due within 4 months of fiscal year end (shortened from 6 months in 2008).

Many investors diversify geographically. Foreign companies' shares can be acquired as American Depositary Receipts (ADRs) on U.S. exchanges, and Form 20-F ensures these companies meet U.S. disclosure standards.

Form 6-K — Foreign Current Report

Used by foreign private issuers to report material information that they have disclosed publicly in their home country, filed with a stock exchange abroad, or distributed to shareholders. It is the foreign equivalent of Form 8-K.

Under SEC rules, foreign private issuers do not file 10-Q quarterly reports. Instead, they use 6-K filings to provide ongoing material information throughout the year. Common 6-K content includes earnings releases, regulatory approvals, and corporate governance changes.

10-K vs. Annual Report — What's the Difference?

Companies file both an annual report and a 10-K with the SEC. While they cover the same fiscal year, they are fundamentally different documents:

Aspect Form 10-K Annual Report
Purpose Legal compliance with SEC regulations Marketing/investor relations communication
Format Standardized SEC format with mandatory Inline XBRL tagging Glossy, illustrated, designed
Length 100+ pages typical 20–50 pages typical
Financials Complete audited statements with all footnotes Summary financials, highlights
Content Risk factors, legal proceedings, MD&A, full segments CEO letter, highlights, strategy overview
Audience Analysts, institutional investors, regulators Shareholders, general public
Required? Required under Rule 14a-3 when proxies are solicited for annual meetings; must be submitted to EDGAR as ARS filing (since Jan 2023) Optional (but common)

Key takeaway: Use the 10-K for fundamental analysis and due diligence. Use the annual report for a quick strategic overview and management perspective.

10-K vs. 10-Q — Annual vs. Quarterly

Aspect Form 10-K Form 10-Q
Frequency Annual (once per year) Quarterly (Q1, Q2, Q3)
Audit status Fully audited financials Unaudited financials
Detail level Comprehensive — all segments and full notes Condensed — key updates, plus mandatory Item 3 (Market Risk) and Item 4 (Controls & Procedures)
Deadline 60–90 days post FY-end 40–45 days post quarter-end
Risk disclosure Full risk factors section Updated risk factors (changes only; SRCs exempt)
Cybersecurity Mandatory Item 1C reporting No standalone item (but must update material changes)

Investor strategy: Use the 10-K for deep annual analysis, then monitor 10-Qs throughout the year to track trends in working capital, receivables, inventory, and legal risks.

10-K vs. 8-K — Scheduled vs. Event-Driven

Aspect Form 10-K Form 8-K
Filing trigger Calendar-based (fiscal year end) Event-based (material event occurs)
Frequency Once per year As needed (could be multiple per year)
Scope Full company overview for entire year Single event or development
Financials Complete annual audited statements Usually none (unless earnings release or significant acquisition — Item 2.01 requires audited financials within 71 calendar days via 8-K/A)
Deadline 60–90 days post FY-end 4 business days after event (cyber incident clock starts from determination of materiality)
Cybersecurity Mandatory Item 1C reporting (strategy/governance) Mandatory Item 1.05 for material incidents

Key insight: The 10-K gives you the full annual picture; the 8-K keeps you informed of developments between annual/quarterly filings. Monitor both for a complete view.

10-Q vs. 8-K — Periodic vs. Immediate

Aspect Form 10-Q Form 8-K
Filing trigger Calendar-based (quarter end) Event-based (material event)
Filing frequency 3 times per year (Q1–Q3) As needed
Content Quarterly financial performance update Disclosure of specific material event
Financials Condensed unaudited quarterly statements Typically none
Timeliness 40–45 day delay from quarter end 4 business days after event (near real-time; cyber incident clock starts from determination of materiality)

Use case: The 10-Q offers a regular quarterly health check; the 8-K provides immediate alerts about significant changes. Together, they minimize information gaps between annual filings.

How to Read a 10-K Like a Professional Investor

The 10-K annual report is the single most important document for understanding a public company. Here's a professional's approach to reading it efficiently:

Step 1: Start With the Business Description (Item 1)

Before diving into numbers, understand what the company actually does. Item 1 describes products, services, customer segments, competitive landscape, and regulatory environment. This context is essential for interpreting the financials.

Step 2: Read Risk Factors (Item 1A)

While many risks are boilerplate, look for company-specific risks. New risks added since the last filing, risks with specific dollar figures, and risks mentioning ongoing litigation are particularly important.

Step 3: Analyze MD&A (Item 7)

Management's Discussion & Analysis is where the company explains its financial results. Look for explanations of revenue growth or decline, margin changes, capital allocation decisions, and forward-looking commentary.

Step 4: Study the Financial Statements

Calculate key ratios: revenue growth, gross margins, operating margins, free cash flow, return on equity, and debt-to-equity. Compare with peers and prior years to identify trends.

Step 5: Read the Footnotes

This is where the most important details hide. Footnotes reveal accounting method changes, pending litigation, off-balance-sheet arrangements, segment detail, and related-party transactions. Never skip this section.

Step 6: Review Item 1C & Exhibit 97

Mandatory since December 2023, Item 1C details cybersecurity risk management processes, strategy, and board oversight. Also, check the cover page for new restatement checkboxes and Exhibit 97 for the company's executive compensation clawback policy.

Understanding Risk Factors in SEC Filings

Every 10-K and S-1 contains a risk factors section that can span 20+ pages. Most investors skip it. That's a mistake.

Separating Signal from Noise

Generic risks ("adverse economic conditions could affect our business") appear in virtually every filing and can be skimmed. Focus instead on:

  • Newly added risks: Compare with prior year's filing. New risks are the most informative.
  • Specific dollar amounts: Risks mentioning specific financial exposure signal genuine concern.
  • Customer concentration: SEC rules mandate disclosing any customer >10% of revenue in Item 1. The real signal is when companies downplay or obscure their true dependence on that major buyer in the risk factors.
  • Regulatory action: Mentions of ongoing SEC investigations, consent orders, or compliance failures.
  • Going concern language: Any mention of "substantial doubt about the company's ability to continue as a going concern" is a major red flag.

How Hedge Funds Use 13F Filings

Form 13F reveals the quarterly equity portfolios of institutional managers with $100M+ in Section 13(f) securities. Here's how to use them effectively:

Tracking Conviction Positions

Look for positions that represent a large percentage of a fund's portfolio — these indicate high conviction. New positions are also meaningful, especially when multiple respected managers initiate the same position simultaneously.

Limitations to Remember

  • 45-day filing delay — positions may have changed
  • Only long equity positions disclosed (Note: mandatory short reporting via Form SHO is coming, but currently delayed to Feb 2028)
  • Snapshot in time — doesn't show intra-quarter trading
  • No disclosure of the investment thesis behind positions

Practical Strategy

Use 13F data as one input among many. Look for consensus among multiple funds, track position changes over several quarters (increasing vs. decreasing conviction), and combine with your own fundamental analysis.

The Most Important Sections of a 10-K

If you only have an hour to review a 10-K, focus on these sections in this order (Note: what was formerly Item 6 "Selected Financial Data" was eliminated in 2021 and is now reserved):

  1. Item 7 — MD&A: Management's narrative about what happened and why. The most qualitative and informative section.
  2. Item 8 — Financial Statements: The audited numbers. Focus on the income statement for profitability trends and the cash flow statement for cash generation.
  3. Footnotes to Financial Statements: Revenue recognition policies, debt structure, lease obligations, pending litigation, and accounting changes.
  4. Item 1A — Risk Factors: Company-specific risks, especially newly added ones.
  5. Item 1C — Cybersecurity: Mandatory since Dec 2023, crucial for evaluating board oversight and cyber strategy.
  6. Item 1 — Business Description: Essential if you're analyzing the company for the first time.
Pro tip: Read the auditor's opinion letter. An "unqualified" (clean) opinion is standard. Any qualifications, explanatory paragraphs (under PCAOB public company standards), or going concern notes deserve immediate attention.

What Is the SEC?

The Securities and Exchange Commission was created through the Securities Exchange Act of 1934 to restore investor confidence after the 1929 crash. It protects investors, maintains fair markets, and facilitates capital formation.

💻

Using EDGAR

EDGAR (Electronic Data Gathering, Analysis, and Retrieval) is the SEC's free system for accessing all filings. Search by company name, ticker, or form type at sec.gov/edgar.

📅

Filing Calendar

Key deadlines: 10-K within 60–90 days of FY-end, 10-Q within 40–45 days of quarter-end, 8-K within 4 business days of event, 13F within 45 days of quarter-end. Missing deadlines triggers NT 10-K / NT 10-Q (Rule 12b-25 notice).

SEC filings are regulatory documents that public companies and securities issuers must submit to the Securities and Exchange Commission. They provide transparency about financial performance, risks, governance, and material events, helping investors make informed decisions.

All SEC filings are available for free on EDGAR (Electronic Data Gathering, Analysis, and Retrieval) at sec.gov/edgar. You can search by company name, CIK number, or ticker symbol. Many brokerage platforms and financial portals like Google Finance also surface SEC filings.

The 10-K is a comprehensive, standardized SEC document with full audited financials, risk factors, legal proceedings, and detailed business descriptions. The annual report is a shorter, glossy marketing document with key highlights, a CEO letter, and summarized financials. The 10-K is required by SEC mandate. The annual report is required under Exchange Act Rule 14a-3 when proxies are solicited and must be electronically submitted to EDGAR as an ARS filing (mandatory since January 2023).

Footnotes reveal accounting methods, off-balance-sheet arrangements, pending litigation, revenue recognition policies, changes in accounting estimates, related-party transactions, and other details that can be red flags. Many accounting irregularities are first visible in the footnotes.

EDGAR Next is the SEC's platform overhaul of the EDGAR filing system, requiring a switch to Login.gov individual accounts with multi-factor authentication (MFA). It also mandates that each filer designate at least two account administrators and complete an annual confirmation process. The mandatory compliance date was September 15, 2025. Un-enrolled filers will lose access and must submit a full new notarized Form ID application subject to SEC staff review after December 19, 2025, for continued EDGAR access.

Companies must file Form 8-K within four business days of a material triggering event such as a merger, bankruptcy, executive departure, or asset acquisition/disposition. Some voluntary disclosures like earnings releases may be filed sooner.
