General Information & Instructions

Quick Summary

  • What: Form 10 is an general.
  • Why: It is required by SEC Mandate to ensure fair disclosure, transparency, and regulatory compliance.
  • When: This is generally an event-based filing. It must be filed within 4 business days.
  • Who: It must be filed by public entities.

Expert Insight (DFIN)

One of the most common SEC filings that publicly traded companies make is Form 10, which is a type of registration statement companies must file in order to be classified as a reporting company under the Exchange Act . Form 10 registers a class of securities under Section 12(g).

When and Why Companies File SEC Form 10

The SEC requires a company to file SEC Form 10 when it has more than $10 million in total assets and more than 500 shareholders. A company with fewer shareholders or assets may voluntarily make this SEC filing, but it is not required.

Given that one purpose of this general form is to register securities, you may be wondering how the SEC Form 10 is different from other required financial statements, such as Form S-3 or Form S-1.

Form S-1, also known as the IPO registration statement, is required when taking a company public via the IPO process. Typically, companies choose an S-1 filing when the overall goal is to raise capital and expand operations through taking a company public.

Form 10 is used by companies at all stages of business development, not just the startup phase. These include spin-offs and carve-outs, among other common triggers. The SEC Form 10 gathers important information about the types of securities being offered, the finances and growth potential for the business and corporate governance, among other things. Companies must also provide supporting documentation to the SEC when they submit Form 10.

The main point to keep in mind is that the information contained in Form 10 gives investors an accurate, up-to-date snapshot of the company offering securities so they can make informed decisions about the investment opportunity.

SEC Form 10 Key Components & Filing Requirements

Form 10 is one of several forms that must be filed before a company can register securities for trading. However, it's insufficient on its own. In other words, companies are also required to make other types of SEC filings in order to comply with regulations. We'll examine some of these other required filings momentarily; for now, let's look at the main components of Form 10.

Best Practices for Preparing and Filing SEC Form 10

Form 10 instructions specify that a company must complete the required paperwork, file it with the SEC and also file supporting documentation, including their Form 10 registration statement, financial statements and all relevant exhibits.

As you might imagine, this is time-consuming! Start early and seek counsel from experienced financial advisors and auditors as well as cross-functional teams within your organization. These professionals can make sure you understand and are prepared to comply with all SEC requirements and support EDGAR Next readiness. They can help gather and validate documentation.

This is also the time to consider technological resources that can streamline the process, which is addressed at length below.

Compliance, Penalties & Post-Filing Obligations

An internal review or audit is essential for checking the accuracy of information, preparing and verifying financial reports. Auditing can identify any discrepancies or errors in a timely manner, so things can be rectified before filing. When this happens, there is less chance of accidental errors in filing.

Another useful tool for compliance with SEC filings is maintaining a calendar that tracks any hard deadlines associated with different required forms.

While Form 10 has no firm deadline and may be voluntary for some filers, other forms have strict deadlines. When those are not met, the company could be penalized or required to pay a fine.

Once a company files their Form 10, other forms then become required. This includes the annual report (Form 10-K), the quarterly report (Form 10-Q) and form 8-K used to report material events.

A place to start is by familiarizing yourself with these forms via the SEC's website, understanding what type of data will be reported on the form and adding filing deadlines to the calendar. Remember, it becomes a post-filing obligation to make these timely filings after the Form 10 is filed.

Once all the necessary information is gathered and a draft of the form is prepared, have a trusted internal team review the draft filing.

Detail orientation is key; any omissions or errors can result in delays. Starting early gives a cushion of time in which gaps can be corrected and errors fixed.

Corporate Governance and SEC Form 10

SEC Form 10 plays an important role in building trust and accountability between companies, investors and regulators.

The Securities Exchange Act of 1934 requires Form 10 to be filed for the purposes of investor transparency. When a company provides this level of operational and financial information up front, it offers investors a sufficient glimpse into its business motives, operations and financial health. This can help to build trust with investors. When this information is required of all companies selling securities, it improves the transparency of the markets as a whole.

From a corporate governance standpoint, forms like these are a way to hold business accountable to a common standard and avoid problems. Simply put, when companies disclose their operations, finances, health and governance practices, stakeholders know what's going on and why — whether the portrait the numbers paint is favorable or unfavorable.

Part II Reference

Core Financial Statements

Based on SEC mandates, this form requires the following audited or unaudited financial comparisons:

Balance Sheet

A statement of the assets, liabilities, and capital of a business at a particular point in time, detailing the balance of income and expenditure over the preceding period.

Required disclosure per Item 8 or Regulation S-X.

Income Statement

A financial statement that reports a company's financial performance over a specific accounting period. Also known as Profit and Loss (P&L).

Required disclosure per Item 8 or Regulation S-X.

Cash Flow Statement

A statement that provides aggregate data regarding all cash inflows a company receives from its ongoing operations and external investment sources.

Required disclosure per Item 8 or Regulation S-X.
